The Problem

The most important transport challenges occur when urban transport systems cannot adequately satisfy the requirements of urban mobility.[1]

- Dr. Jean-Paul Rodrigue

Manual Challenges

How people use transportation is changing. The percentage of citizens owning a car in Oslo is declining.[2] Manually driven cars are ineffective and prone to accidents due to inattentiveness and other human errors [3]. Queues in rush hour leads to increased emission and pollution. [4] In addition, the average car is parked 96,8 % of the time, [5] making private ownership less sustainable both for the environment and for the owner.

A car is parked 96.8% of the time

Automatic Future

A revolution of fully automated cars is due, and by 2025 there will be approximately 8 million autonomous or semi-autonomous vehicles on the road. [6] The need for a dedicated plan arises. If left to private profiteers, the Norwegian government might lose the opportunity to make sure that the solution will be accessible, safe, and sustainable for all its citizens, while extracting socio-economic value for the population at large. Norway cannot afford to be reactive and miss out on the opportunity to create a safer, cheaper, and more sustainable transport system for its citizens. [7]
